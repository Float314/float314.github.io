A warranty is basically a developer saying, “If this thing breaks, I’ve got your back,”
which sounds nice until you remember software is held together by late-night decisions and
vibes; so most licenses politely avoid that commitment and go with “as is,” meaning you get
the software in whatever mysterious state it currently exists—working, broken, or philosophically
confused—because guaranteeing it would mean promising control over every computer, setup, and
chaotic user choice on Earth, which is about as realistic as guaranteeing your code won’t suddenly
fail right before a demo; so instead of signing up for that level of responsibility, developers
hand it over with a quiet shrug and a legal sentence that translates to, “good luck, and may the
bugs be ever in your favor.”

“No liability” is the even more dramatic cousin of “no warranty,” where the developer gently but
firmly declares, “If this software ruins your day, your files, or somehow your entire week, that’s...
not on me,” because once code leaves their hands it enters a wild ecosystem of strange devices, cursed
configurations, and users who click things with confidence but no context; taking responsibility for
all possible outcomes would mean being legally tied to every glitch, crash, or unexpected chaos event,
which is less “writing code” and more “signing up to be blamed for the universe,” so licenses include
no liability as a kind of protective spell—handing you the software like a mysterious artifact and quietly
adding, “use it wisely, because if it turns your project into modern art, we were never here.”

And while I was writing this document, I somehow ended up hearing about Yakutsk, which feels like the kind

about discoveries in the South Sakha Region makes it all sound like a story waiting to happen. It’s strange

Back to my document, this software comes with absolutely no guarantees whatsoever, as you may have already suspected. 
It exists in its current form, doing whatever it decides to do on any given day. And as mentioned above, there is no
liability here—none at all, not even by accident. If something goes wrong, it’s just one of those moments where reality
shrugs and moves on.

Also a recap, “no liability” definitely doesn’t mean you get a free pass to go make a virus—that’s
very much still a terrible idea with real consequences. Speaking of viruses, did you know about WannaCry
ransomware attack, which spread across the world and locked people out of their own computers, hitting hospitals,
businesses, and governments and causing massive disruption and financial damage. It basically held systems hostage
until payments were made, which is about as chaotic as software gets. No wonder mankind has a habit of building things
that can go from clever to catastrophic real fast.

Why does mankind make software that steals data? Honestly, most of the time it comes down to money, power,
or both—data can be sold, used for scams, targeted ads, identity theft, or even corporate spying, 
so for some people it’s just an easy (and illegal) way to profit. I don’t have any interest in 
stealing other people’s data either, and yeah, in fields like AI, data *is* needed to train models—but 
that’s usually supposed to be collected legally and ethically, not grabbed in shady ways. The confusion 
comes from the fact that even companies not focused on AI still want data because it helps them understand
users, improve products, personalize content, and, most importantly, make more money through advertising or 
analytics. So while “train AI” is one reason, the bigger picture is that data has basically become one of 
the most valuable resources in the digital world—and wherever something is valuable, some people will try 
to get it the wrong way.

Going back to the main topic, you aren’t allowed to distribute a closed-source version of this
code, and that part is very much intentional. Open-source developers don’t spend hours debugging, 
building, and refining something just to watch it get locked up behind a corporate wall. It’s less 
about control and more about basic respect for the work that went into it. Nobody wants their blood
and sweat repackaged, rebranded, and sold without giving anything back. So this rule exists to make 
sure the effort stays open and doesn’t get quietly turned into someone else’s private asset.

Capitalism in the open-source world shouldn’t be allowed, at least not in the way it tries to
turn everything into profit first and purpose second. Open source was meant to be simple: 
share knowledge, improve together, and let ideas flow freely without a price tag slapped on 
every corner. When money becomes the main focus, that simplicity starts to fade, and collaboration 
turns into quiet competition. Sure, capitalism works in countries where economies need structure 
and growth, but in tech spaces built on openness, it often feels out of place. Instead of 
encouraging contribution, it can shift the mindset toward ownership and control.

As it stands, we’ve reached a point where developers are reverse-engineering things just to understand 
or make systems work better together. Some people say that’s illegal, but in many cases it feels 
more like solving a tough math problem than committing a crime. It’s curiosity mixed with necessity, 
especially when documentation is missing or intentionally hidden. Comparing it to 9th or 10th grade 
math isn’t that far off—it’s about breaking things down and figuring out how they work. The real issue 
isn’t the act itself, but where the line is drawn between learning and misuse, and that line isn’t 
always as clear as people pretend it is.

But that doesn’t mean you yourself can’t distribute programs—you absolutely can, and that’s
kind of the point. If you don’t feel like putting in too much effort on promotion, you can
even lean on something like Google Ads to do the heavy lifting. You can earn money from your program
while letting ads bring in users, even if it feels like taking the easy route. Of course, that also 
means giving a cut of your earnings to the platform running those ads. In the end, it becomes a 
trade-off where you gain reach and potential income, while they gain data and revenue from your spending.

Changes in this document are expected, and if they occur, we’ll include a version number to keep things
clear and organized. We will not collect or steal user data, simply because we have no interest in doing so.
In the open-source world, transparency is a core idea, so any data-related behavior would be visible to 
everyone anyway.That said, open source doesn’t automatically mean zero data collection—it just means 
nothing is hidden.The goal here is to stay transparent, minimal, and respectful of user trust.
